CHAPTER 01

What is ethics? Not a list of prohibitions

Singer clears away three myths first. Ethics is not a set of puritanical bans designed to stop fun; that is just one particular moral code mistaken for morality itself. Ethics is not noble in theory but useless in practice, since the whole point of an ethical judgement is to guide action, so one that fails in practice has a theoretical defect too. And ethics is not a short list of simple rules like "do not lie," because real life is too complex for that.

An ethical judgment that is no good in practice must suffer from a theoretical defect as well.

So where does morality come from? Singer surveys the options. The divine command theory says acts are right because God commands them, but Plato refuted a version of this two thousand years ago: if the gods approve of good actions because they are good, then it is not the approval that makes them good. Kant, a devout Christian, still scorned acting morally out of self-interested hope of reward.

Reason, not just feeling

Singer's key move: ethics is universalizable. To say an act is right is to claim it from a standpoint beyond my own wants. "Act only on that maxim through which you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law" (Kant). Singer argues this universal aspect gives a persuasive, though not conclusive, reason to take a broadly utilitarian position: once I think beyond myself, I weigh everyone's interests, which looks a lot like maximising what is good for all affected.

A consequentialist starts with goals, not rules. So: is lying always wrong?
Yes, lying breaks a rule, so it is always wrong
It depends on the consequences in the situation
CHAPTER 02

Equality is a moral principle, not a fact

Here is the move most people miss. We cannot ground equality on the factual claim that everyone is equally talented, because they plainly are not. Singer shows why that grounding fails with a chilling thought experiment: imagine sorting people by IQ into a slave-owning class, free citizens, and slaves. If equality rested on "differences cut across race and sex," this hierarchy, based on real differences, would have no answer. So equality cannot rest on facts about ability.

Equality is a basic ethical principle, not an assertion of fact.

Instead Singer offers the principle of equal consideration of interests: we give equal weight to the like interests of all affected. Crucially this is minimal, it does not require equal treatment. Sometimes equal consideration leads to unequal treatment.

Sex differences and the opportunity gap

Singer runs the same logic on sex. Even granting a biological story about, say, visual-spatial ability, the biology cannot carry the weight people put on it. The "advantaged X chromosome" theory would predict roughly twice as many men as women at the top of spatial-skill fields. Yet in most countries there are not twice as many men as women in architecture and engineering, but at least ten times as many. The biology cannot explain that gap, which means something else is doing most of the work.

Even if one accepts biological explanations for the patterning of these abilities, women still do not have the same opportunities as men to make the most of the abilities they have.

Two further points seal it. First, whatever the origin of psychological differences between the sexes, social conditioning can emphasise or soften them. Second, the abilities themselves are not fixed: where women are raised to be independent, their visual-spatial ability is much higher than where they are kept at home and dependent on men. So the trait we measure is partly produced by the very inequality it is used to excuse.

My take

I found this genuinely clever. It's the same structural move as the IQ argument but harder to wriggle out of: even if you grant the biology, the 10x gap is far too big for biology to explain, so the opportunity argument survives intact. It reframes "innate difference" as partly an artefact of conditioning, which neatly defuses the usual biological-determinist objection without having to deny biology outright.

Equality, IQ, and affirmative action

Because equality is a principle not a fact, Singer argues it survives even if there turned out to be group differences in measured IQ: equal status never depended on intelligence in the first place. "Racists who maintain the contrary are in peril of being forced to kneel before the next genius they encounter." He extends equal consideration to disability, and discusses affirmative action (the Bakke case): preferring a disadvantaged applicant can be justified either because standardised tests understate a disadvantaged student's real ability, or because there is no intrinsic right to admission in the first place, so using race as one criterion is not the injustice that excluding someone from a right would be.

My take

I think Singer leans too hard on "no inherent right to admission" to defend reverse discrimination. He flags the risk himself, that graduates from preferred groups may get labelled inferior, and that once we approve racial quotas nothing stops them being turned against minorities later. I want to hold both of those at once rather than treat the case as settled.

CHAPTER 03

Equality for animals?

If equal consideration of interests is the right basis for how we treat each other, Singer argues we cannot consistently stop at the species boundary. Bentham's line is the hinge: the question is not "can they reason?" nor "can they talk?" but "can they suffer?" A stone has no interests because it cannot suffer; a being that can suffer has an interest in not suffering, and that interest deserves equal weight.

The question is not, can they reason? nor, can they talk? but, can they suffer?

To weight a being's interests less simply because it is not human, Singer says, is speciesism, structurally like racism or sexism. He notes the anatomical and behavioural evidence that animals feel pain is strong, and that none of those grounds hold for plants, which lack a centralised nervous system. He argues this rules out factory-farmed meat and trivial animal testing like the Draize cosmetics test.

My take

Singer claims animal flesh is unnecessary for health, and I'm not sure I fully agree there. I also think his "it's not wrong to interfere with a natural process" line is weak as stated: we interfere with natural processes precisely to reduce suffering, so the appeal to what's "natural" cuts both ways and can't do the work he wants it to.

CHAPTER 04

What's wrong with killing?

Why does human life have special value? Singer separates "member of the species Homo sapiens" from person, in Locke's sense: a being with reason and reflection that can think of itself as the same self over time. Fletcher's "indicators of humanhood" (self-awareness, a sense of past and future, the capacity to relate to others) track personhood, not species.

This matters because, on a preference view, the wrong of killing a person is that it thwarts their preference to go on living, a preference that requires conceiving of yourself as existing over time. Tooley's hard claim: a being that has never had the concept of a continuing self cannot have an interest in continuing to exist. Singer pairs this with autonomy: killing someone who does not choose to die is the gravest violation of the most fundamental choice there is.

My take

This is the part of the book I find most confronting. The personhood criterion implies some humans (newborns, the severely cognitively impaired) score lower than some animals, and Singer follows that exactly where most of us recoil. I can track the force of the "indicators" approach without endorsing where it lands, and I think it carries real danger alongside its real logical pull.

The case of Baby Andrew

Singer grounds the abstraction in a real case. Despite both parents asking for "no heroics," doctors used the full force of modern medicine to keep Baby Andrew Stinson alive for nearly six months. He had periodic fits, and by the end it was clear that if he survived at all he would be seriously and permanently impaired. He was also in considerable pain: at one point a doctor said it must "hurt like hell" every time Andrew drew a breath. Singer uses "the long dying of Baby Andrew" to ask whether prolonging a life can itself be the harm, and whether more treatment is always the more compassionate choice.

My take

This case stuck with me because it flips the usual intuition that doing everything to preserve life is automatically the kind thing. Andrew's suffering was actively extended against his parents' wishes. It makes the quality-of-life argument feel a lot less theoretical, even if I still find Singer's broader conclusions in this chapter hard to sit with.

Two utilitarianisms

Singer flags a fork that returns in Ch 5: the total view (maximise total happiness, which counts creating happy beings as good) versus the prior-existence view (only count beings who already exist or will independently of our choice). Most people's intuition, that a couple is not obliged to have a child even if it would be happy, fits the prior-existence view.

CHAPTER 05

Taking life: animals, persons, and replaceability

Singer brings the threads together. Some animals show real evidence of self-consciousness: Julia the chimp solved problems without language; de Waal's chimps plan and cooperate. So some non-human animals may be persons, and some humans are not. When in real doubt whether a being is a person, Singer says give it the benefit of the doubt.

The hardest idea here is replaceability. Leslie Stephen's dark quip: "the pig has a stronger interest than anyone in the demand for bacon," because without the demand the pig would never exist at all. If a being is merely sentient and not a person, the total view suggests it can be "replaced" by another happy being. Parfit-style cases then bite: if creating a miserable being is bad, why is creating a happy one not good and obligatory? Pushed all the way, the moral-ledger model leads to the anti-natalist conclusion that it would have been better if none of us had been born, which Singer takes as a sign the ledger model fails.

Singer's tentative landing: sentience is enough to place a being inside equal consideration of interests, but it does not by itself give a being a personal interest in continuing to live. That distinction does a lot of work across all five chapters.

CHAPTER 06

Taking life: the embryo and the fetus

The problem Singer sets up: there is no obvious sharp line dividing the fertilised egg from the adult. The conservative argument is clean. It is wrong to kill an innocent human being; a fetus is an innocent human being; therefore it is wrong to kill a fetus. Singer attacks the second premise by testing the usual dividing lines, and arguing none of them does the work people want.

Proposed lineSinger's objection
BirthThe fetus is the same entity inside or outside the womb, same features, same capacity to feel.
ViabilityIt moves with medical technology and location. The New York vs New Guinea case shows a trip cannot change the fetus's claim to life.
QuickeningWhen the mother first feels movement is a fact about her, not a change in the fetus's moral status.
ConsciousnessSinger takes this most seriously: before the cortex supports experience, there is no sentient being to wrong.

Singer adds Judith Jarvis Thomson's famous violinist case (you wake up plugged into a stranger who needs your body for nine months to survive) which defends abortion via bodily rights even if the fetus is a person. But a utilitarian who rejects Thomson's rights framework rejects that argument too, and instead weighs interests: a woman's serious interests normally override the rudimentary interests of even a conscious fetus, though if a method causes the fetus to suffer it should be avoided.

On the argument from potential, Singer's reply is that a potential person does not have the rights of an actual one. The lab embryo sharpens this: before about 14 days it can still split into twins, so it is not yet even an individual (the "where did Mary go?" puzzle), and no one in the pro-life movement tries to rescue eggs and sperm despite their potential.

My take

For me, abortion seems morally permissible only before the central nervous system develops, that is, before there is a sentient being who can be harmed. That lines up with Singer's consciousness criterion rather than the bodily-rights route. Once there is a being that can experience, the calculation changes for me.

The infanticide objection

Singer accepts the most notorious consequence head-on: if birth is not a morally sharp line, then the intrinsic wrongness of killing a late fetus and a newborn are not markedly different. He argues a newborn is not yet a person in the self-aware, future-oriented sense, that valuing infants for being "small and cute" is like valuing a baby seal over a gorilla, and that absolute protection of infant life is a distinctively Christian inheritance rather than a universal value. He concedes birth may still be the right legal line because it is sharp and clear.

My take

I think it's worth taking Singer's most controversial views seriously rather than flinching from them, having uncomfortable or even seemingly insane positions on the table is part of doing philosophy honestly. That said, I disagree with the idea that parents should be able to end a disabled child's life. A disabled child who could live is not a problem to be solved by killing. But I draw a distinction Singer tends to blur: where a child is on life support, is not self-conscious, and is likely to be left suffering if kept alive, then withdrawing that life support should be a decision for the parents, not the hospital or the law. That is about not prolonging futile suffering and about who holds the decision, which is different from actively ending a viable life because it is judged not worth living.

CHAPTER 07

Taking life: humans, and euthanasia

"Euthanasia" means a gentle death, now used for killing those incurably ill and in great pain. Singer splits it three ways, and the distinction is the whole chapter:

Voluntary: carried out at the request of the person killed
Non-voluntary: the person cannot understand the choice (e.g. a severely impaired infant)
Involuntary: against the person's will

Singer's case for voluntary euthanasia rests on the same machinery as the rest of the book: a self-conscious being can prefer not to go on, and autonomy (respect for a rational agent's own choice about the most fundamental matter there is) gives strong reason to honour that request. He cites the Kevorkian / Janet Adkins case. For infants he argues non-voluntary euthanasia, on the total view, treats the newborn as replaceable, since no infant has as strong a claim to life as a being that sees itself as existing over time.

He closes by weighing the sanctity-of-life risk honestly: abandoning the traditional ethic carries a small but real risk of unwanted consequences, set against the tangible harm of needlessly prolonging misery. He bets a clearer, interest-based ethic is the firmer long-run ground.

My take

I support voluntary euthanasia: if autonomy means anything, it has to include a competent person's choice about their own death, and refusing that can prolong real suffering. Non-voluntary cases are harder, but I don't reject them outright. Where someone (an infant or otherwise) is not self-conscious and is only being kept biologically alive into ongoing suffering, I think withdrawing treatment can be the humane choice, and the call should sit with the family rather than the hospital or the law. What I will not accept is the leap from "withdraw futile treatment" to "actively end a life judged not worth living." That line matters to me even where Singer treats it as porous.

CHAPTER 08

Rich and poor

The chapter that launched the effective-altruism movement. Singer defines absolute poverty as life at the very margin of existence, lacking the income to meet basic biological needs, and notes it costs countless lives, especially infants. By the same measure most people in wealthy nations are absolutely affluent. He even notes that grain and soy fed to animals would, redistributed, more than end world hunger.

By not giving more than we do, people in rich countries are allowing those in poor countries to suffer and die.

The core argument is an analogy: if you can prevent something very bad without sacrificing anything of comparable moral importance, you ought to do it. Letting a child drown to keep your shoes dry is monstrous; Singer argues distance and number do not change the underlying duty. The hard question is the demandingness: how far does "comparable moral importance" cut into an ordinary life?

How far does the duty go?

give nothinggive until it hurts
My take

I agree with the duty. The drowning-child analogy genuinely lands for me, distance and numbers really should not dissolve an obligation we would feel instantly in person. Where I am unsure is how far it goes. Singer's stronger version asks us to give until we reach the point of comparable moral sacrifice, and I do not yet know whether the right answer is that demanding or whether a more sustainable, less totalising duty is defensible. I am settled on the principle, still working out the limit.

CHAPTERS 09 TO 12 · SUMMARY

The chapters I haven't read yet

I ran out of time before my deadline at Chapter 8, so the four below are concise summaries of Singer's arguments rather than worked-through notes with my own responses. I'll come back and add my views once I've actually read them. Positions here are Singer's.

Chapter 9 · Climate change

Singer treats climate change as a global ethics problem about fairly sharing a limited resource: the atmosphere's capacity to absorb greenhouse gases. He weighs several principles for dividing the remaining carbon budget, the polluter-pays / historical-responsibility view (those who emitted most in the past owe most now), and an equal-per-person share of the atmosphere. Because rich nations have used far more than a per-capita share, he argues they carry the heavier obligation to cut emissions and to help poorer nations. It extends the Chapter 8 logic: if distance and borders don't dissolve duties, neither does the diffuseness of climate harm.

Chapter 10 · The environment

Here Singer asks what we owe the natural world and why. He is critical of a purely human-centred ("anthropocentric") ethic that values nature only as a resource, and extends his interests-based view: sentient animals count for their own sake. He examines, but is cautious about, "deeper" green positions that grant intrinsic value to non-sentient things like species, ecosystems, or wilderness, since on his framework value ultimately tracks the interests of beings who can experience. He still defends strong reasons to protect the environment, including duties to future generations.

Chapter 11 · Civil disobedience, violence and terrorism

When, if ever, is it justified to break the law for a moral cause? Singer argues that in a democracy there's a strong but not absolute reason to obey the law. Where lawful means are exhausted and the stakes are serious, civil disobedience (open, non-violent, accepting the legal consequences) can be justified by its likely consequences. He's far more restrictive about violence and terrorism, judging them by their results and the dangerous precedents they set, which sets a very high bar that is rarely met.

Chapter 12 · Why act morally?

The closing question: once we know what ethics asks, why be ethical at all, especially when acting morally cuts against self-interest? Singer resists grounding it purely in self-interest. Instead he argues that taking up "the ethical point of view," reasoning from a standpoint beyond one's own preferences, gives life a kind of meaning and coherence that narrow self-interest cannot. It loops back to Chapter 1: ethics is universal by nature, and living that way is a defensible answer to "how should I live?"

A CLOSING NOTE

On controversy in ethics

While Singer's views are controversial, especially those involving infanticide and disability, and sometimes seem absurd or outside what is socially acceptable, that kind of discussion must be allowed in the realm of academia. It is how we further the conversation and decide, as a society, where our ethical boundaries actually lie.

Engaging an argument is not the same as endorsing its conclusion. Several of my own positions on this page push back hard against Singer, but I think the right response to an uncomfortable argument is to meet it, not to refuse to hear it.

"Sentience suffices to place a being within the sphere of equal consideration of interests."
